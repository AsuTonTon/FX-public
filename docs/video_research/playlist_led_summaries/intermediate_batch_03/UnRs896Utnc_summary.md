## 2. Compact paraphrased lesson summary

This lesson surveys the speaker's preferred chart-pattern families and repeatedly argues that patterns matter only when they are tied to context, entry sequence, stop placement, and a realistic target model. The lecture narrows the set to patterns the speaker claims to actually use: double bottom/top, head and shoulders, triangles, wedges, and flags.

For double bottom/top, the lesson prefers a diagonal neckline as the main working line, with horizontal levels kept as a secondary reference. Entry is delayed until a neckline break confirms and either the break candle closes or a return move gives a cleaner trigger. Stop placement is anchored around the second point of the pattern, while first target logic leans on Elliott-wave projection or a measured move.

Head and shoulders is treated as a contextual reversal pattern rather than a shape-recognition shortcut. The speaker says it should be anticipated from wave context and top/bottom structure, not declared simply because three bumps resemble the pattern. The same broader point reappears in triangles, wedges, and flags: pattern quality depends on trend strength, location, line cleanliness, and expected continuation behavior.

Triangles receive the most caution because false breaks are described as common. Wedges are presented as cleaner and more teachable when they appear near tops or bottoms with clear multi-touch geometry. Flags are continuation patterns, so the strength of the prior trend and of the surrounding environment determines whether the breakout should be trusted.
